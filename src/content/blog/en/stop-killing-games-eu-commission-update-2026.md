> Checked on 2026-06-18. This article only summarizes information that can be verified from EU pages, the European Commission press release, Stop Killing Games' official site, and recent reporting. Unpublished meeting details and full attendee lists are not treated as facts.

The EU procedure connected to Stop Killing Games is formally called **Stop Destroying Videogames**, a European Citizens' Initiative. Its core demand is that games sold or licensed in the European Union should not be remotely disabled into an unplayable state when official publisher support ends.

As of 2026-06-18, the latest situation has two separate tracks:

1. On 2026-06-16, the European Commission said it would not propose a new legal obligation requiring games to remain playable after they stop being provided commercially.
2. Stop Killing Games says it will continue through the European Parliament, especially by pushing related language into the Digital Fairness Act.

## Confirmed Timeline

### January 2026: Signatures Cleared Verification

The Stop Destroying Videogames ECI cleared the threshold and moved into the EU process. Recent reporting and campaign materials cite **1,294,188** verified signatures, above the one-million requirement and across enough member states.

### April 16, 2026: European Parliament Public Hearing

The campaign was heard in a European Parliament public hearing. This was not a legislative victory by itself, but it moved the shutdown of remotely dependent games from a community issue into a formal policy discussion.

### June 3, 2026: Ubisoft / Video Games Europe Meeting Controversy

Stop Killing Games alleged that Ubisoft CEO Yves Guillemot attended an invitation-only meeting connected to the European Commission and hosted by **Video Games Europe**, while Stop Killing Games was not invited.

Precision matters here: what is publicly verifiable is the Stop Killing Games allegation and press reporting around it. Public information clearly names Ubisoft's CEO, Video Games Europe, and the European Commission context. I have not found a complete reliable public attendee list, so it should not be written as "all major publishers secretly attended."

The controversy is about timing and balance. The meeting reportedly happened about two weeks before the Commission's expected response to the ECI. Stop Killing Games argues that industry representatives had access while the campaign did not receive equal representation.

### June 16, 2026: European Commission Response

The European Commission press release `IP_26_1369` says that, at this stage, the Commission cannot propose a legal obligation to keep games playable after commercial provision ends. The reason given includes existing intellectual property rights and exclusive rights under EU copyright law.

The Commission did not accept the campaign's central request for binding legislation. Instead, it proposed:

- meeting with industry and consumer representatives
- drawing up an industry code of conduct for managing video game end of life
- promoting a consumer-rights awareness campaign

In short, the Commission is currently favoring soft rules and consumer information rather than a hard requirement for offline modes, private server tools, or independently playable versions.

## Does This End Stop Killing Games?

No.

Stop Killing Games' response is that the Commission is not the only path. According to reporting from 2026-06-16, Ross Scott argues that the campaign has made progress in the European Parliament and will keep pushing through amendments to the **Digital Fairness Act**.

That shifts the next phase from "will the Commission propose a new law?" to:

- whether the European Parliament supports amendments
- whether the Digital Fairness Act can include rules about game shutdowns and digital product durability
- whether the code of conduct becomes meaningful or remains voluntary language
- whether consumer-rights awareness leads to clearer storefront warnings about what will stop working

## The Role of Video Games Europe

Video Games Europe represents the European games industry. On the Stop Killing Games issue, it has raised industry-side concerns such as:

- long-term server maintenance costs
- development cost of offline modes or private server support
- legal risks around fan-operated servers or preserved versions
- copyright, licensed content, third-party middleware, brand, and safety issues
- the difficulty of applying one rule to very different game architectures

Those concerns are part of the policy debate. Stop Killing Games' counterargument is that the campaign does not demand eternal publisher-operated servers or endless updates. It asks for reasonable end-of-life measures so sold games are not remotely turned into unusable products.

## Current Takeaways

1. **The European Commission did not accept immediate binding legislation.** That is the latest formal result from 2026-06-16.
2. **The Ubisoft / Video Games Europe invitation-only meeting triggered a procedural fairness dispute.** But the full attendee list and meeting contents are not public.
3. **Stop Killing Games is not over.** The focus moves to the European Parliament and the Digital Fairness Act.
4. **The campaign is not asking publishers to run servers forever.** Its stated goal is a reasonably playable state after shutdown.
5. **The next important items are DFA amendments and the code of conduct.** Whether anything real changes depends on the wording and enforceability.

## Why This Matters

This is not only about one Ubisoft game. The real issue is what "buying" a digital game means. If a game is sold like a product, but the publisher can later shut down servers and make even single-player or core content unplayable, did the customer buy a product, a service, a rental, or revocable access?

The Commission has not given Stop Killing Games the answer it wanted, but the issue is now part of the European policy agenda. The next major test is whether the Digital Fairness Act and the European Parliament turn the idea of "reasonable post-shutdown playability" into concrete rules.
