> Checked on 2026-06-25. This article is based on Valve and Steam pages, Valve engineer interviews, and recent reporting. Prices, availability, pre-order rules, and hardware details can still change, so always confirm the official Steam page before buying.

Valve's new [Steam Machine](https://store.steampowered.com/sale/steammachine?l=english) triggered a strong reaction once pricing started circulating. The Verge reported a **$1,049 512GB model** and a **$1,349 2TB model**. Adding the Steam Controller brings another **$79**. For many players, that is far above the mental price range of a living-room game box and much closer to a small gaming PC.

But reducing the whole issue to "Valve wants a huge margin" would be too simple. The public information points to three overlapping realities: PC hardware components are expensive, Valve says it is not subsidizing this device like a traditional console, and the Steam Machine is still a PC rather than a closed console platform.

## The Steam Machine Is a SteamOS PC, Not a Traditional Console

Valve's official Steam Machine page presents it as a way to bring Steam games to the big screen. The page says it has more than six times the horsepower of Steam Deck and targets a 4K / 60 FPS living-room experience with FSR. That may look like a console pitch, but Valve's own positioning is closer to a small SteamOS PC.

That distinction matters. Xbox and PlayStation can use a closed platform, licensing, subscriptions, digital-store revenue, first-party content, and long hardware cycles to balance hardware pricing. In some phases, console makers can sell hardware near cost or below cost because the platform can recover value later. Valve says it does not want to take that route here.

The Verge reported Valve's explanation for not subsidizing the Steam Machine: selling hardware below cost tends to push companies toward a closed system where users are locked into content and services. Valve's position is that the openness of the PC ecosystem matters, and Steam Machine should be one PC option among many, not a mandatory gateway.

In other words, Steam Machine is not a "Valve PS5." It is closer to a standardized, SteamOS-powered small PC designed for the living room. That makes it harder to price like a subsidized console.

## Memory and Storage Prices Put Valve in a Weak Position

The other major part of the price debate is that the hardware market in 2026 is not the market Valve designed around earlier.

Tom's Hardware interviewed Valve engineers Pierre-Loup Griffais and Yazan Aldehayyat and reported that the original Steam Machine design was based on memory and storage prices from roughly two years earlier. Griffais said the final price reflects the wider market situation more than the product itself.

The clearest detail came from the Gamers Nexus interview. The Verge and GamesRadar both summarized the same point: when Gamers Nexus asked whether Valve could lock in contracts with memory suppliers, Griffais said there were no contracts. Suppliers give Valve a price and a quantity roughly every month, and Valve can only say yes or no. If Valve says no, those suppliers may not talk to them again.

That does not prove every hidden reason behind the pricing. What it does confirm is that Valve did not have the kind of long-term stable memory agreement many players might imagine. The most reasonable public explanation is that AI servers, data centers, and very large buyers are putting extreme pressure on memory and storage supply, leaving gaming hardware with less leverage.

So the phrase "Steam Machine is too small to negotiate like a giant buyer" needs precision. Valve is not a small company. But compared with AI data-center purchasing, a living-room gaming PC may not have the volume or priority needed to demand long-term low-price supply contracts. Griffais's comments at least show the result: Valve was exposed to monthly supplier pricing and quota decisions.

## AI Demand Is Squeezing Gaming Hardware

Generative AI and data-center expansion have reshaped memory demand. Even if Steam Machine does not use HBM, the broader supply chain is connected: wafer capacity, packaging, NAND, DDR, SSD controllers, supplier scheduling, and priority customers all influence consumer hardware prices.

GamesRadar's coverage noted that generative AI companies have been buying up memory in advance, and memory suppliers have strong incentives to prioritize large AI-related customers. This is why players have started using terms like RAMpocalypse or RAMflation. The issue is not only that Steam Machine is expensive; the wider PC hardware market has become more expensive.

Valve's position is awkward. It is a huge gaming platform company, but in physical hardware procurement it is not a long-running console manufacturer with massive fixed hardware volume, and it is not an AI data-center buyer. If memory pricing moves monthly and Valve is also avoiding a closed-platform subsidy model, the retail price has less room to hide the component shock.

## Why Xbox and PS5 Comparisons Are Tricky

Players naturally compare Steam Machine with PS5, Xbox Series X, and PS5 Pro. They all connect to a TV and run games. But the comparison has traps.

First, console pricing has also moved. Sony said in an April 2025 PlayStation Blog post that it was raising PS5 prices in parts of Europe, Australia, and New Zealand because of a challenging economic environment, high inflation, and exchange-rate changes. Microsoft has also adjusted Xbox hardware and Game Pass pricing in recent years, and in April 2026 announced a Game Pass Ultimate price drop back to $22.99 while changing some release benefits.

Those examples show that console pricing is not only about bill-of-materials cost. It also reflects platform strategy, subscription pricing, first-party content spending, acquisition costs, exchange rates, tariffs, and company policy choices. Using Xbox or PS5 pricing to say Steam Machine must be cheaper is imprecise; blaming the full Steam Machine price only on Valve greed is also too shallow.

The key difference is business model. Traditional consoles can offset hardware through a closed ecosystem, game sales, subscriptions, licensing, and long-term scale. Steam Machine is an open PC ecosystem device running SteamOS, and Valve says it does not want to trade cheaper hardware for a closed platform.

## SteamOS Can Run on Other PCs, and Valve Does Not Object

One of the most important differences is that Valve is not locking SteamOS only to its own hardware.

The official SteamOS page describes SteamOS as Valve's Linux-based operating system, optimized for gaming while keeping PC performance and flexibility. It is the official operating system of Steam Deck, and Valve says support for more devices is expanding.

Tom's Guide, citing The Verge's interview, reported that Griffais said SteamOS 3.8 allows users to build their own Steam Machine-like PC from parts. Valve is also improving compatibility with more desktop hardware, including future Nvidia support. There are still caveats: the experience is currently better suited to a TV-connected, single-drive system rather than a dual-boot setup, and a self-built device may not have the same HDMI-CEC, controller, or installer polish as official hardware.

That means Valve's message is not "only our box counts." It is closer to pushing a broader SteamOS ecosystem: buy Valve's finished hardware if you want the integrated version, or build your own if you accept the trade-offs.

## Is a DIY Steam Machine Actually Cheaper?

After the price reaction, many PC players started asking whether they could simply build their own Steam Machine. That is a reasonable instinct because PC users often compare prebuilt hardware against DIY parts.

But once the parts list is real, the answer is less simple. Steam Machine combines a compact enclosure, living-room noise targets, a semi-custom AMD CPU/GPU design, memory, SSD storage, power delivery, cooling, wireless, industrial design, OS integration, and support. Matching only the broad performance target with desktop parts is not the same as matching size, acoustics, power behavior, and usability.

The more important point is that 2026 memory and storage prices are already high. The Verge reported Valve saying the price is basically component and manufacturing cost, with margins kept close to cost. Another Verge report on memory procurement connected the 512GB and 2TB Steam Machine prices to the jump in memory and storage cost.

DIY can still make sense, but not always because it is dramatically cheaper. It makes the most sense if:

1. You already own useful hardware and mainly want to install SteamOS.
2. You are willing to trade size, appearance, noise, or living-room integration for performance.
3. You want to choose your own GPU, SSD, RAM, case, and cooling rather than buy Valve's integrated design.

If you are buying every part new and trying to match the official Steam Machine's compactness, quiet operation, and living-room experience, the savings may be smaller than expected.

## What the Price Debate Really Shows

The Steam Machine controversy is not only about one product being expensive. It is a snapshot of the pressure hitting gaming hardware in 2026.

Players want the price of a console because it looks like a living-room console. Valve treats it as a PC because it runs SteamOS, lives in the open PC ecosystem, and is not meant to be a subsidized gateway into a closed platform. Players expect Valve to have strong memory pricing power; Griffais's interview suggests that, under current AI-driven supply pressure, Valve's hardware team is still exposed to supplier terms.

That is why both statements can be true at the same time: Steam Machine is expensive for players, and Valve may still not be making a large margin on it. For a buyer, $1,049 is high. For Valve, that price may reflect component costs, manufacturing costs, supply constraints, and the decision not to subsidize.

## Buying Notes

1. Do not judge Steam Machine only as a console; it is a compact SteamOS PC.
2. If you only want the cheapest way to play games on a TV, PS5, Xbox, a used PC, or a normal desktop may make more sense.
3. If you value your Steam library, SteamOS, a living-room interface, lower maintenance, and official integration, Steam Machine becomes more interesting.
4. Building your own Steam Machine is possible, but it may not be much cheaper when RAM and SSD prices are high.
5. SteamOS on generic PC hardware still has compatibility and installer caveats, especially around dual boot, HDMI-CEC, controller integration, and Nvidia support.
6. Prices, availability, and bundle rules can still change, so confirm the Steam page before buying.

## Conclusion

The anger around Steam Machine pricing is understandable because the device is not cheap. But judging it only against PS5 or Xbox misses the PC nature of the product, SteamOS openness, weaker component leverage, and the memory price shock created by the AI boom.

A more accurate summary is this: Steam Machine is an idealistic SteamOS PC launching into a bad hardware market cycle. It may not be the cheap mass-market console many players wanted, but it does show Valve's direction. SteamOS is no longer only for Steam Deck or one official box; it is slowly becoming a gaming platform that can spread across PCs, laptops, and living-room devices.
